Interpolating Functions

Consider a function f(x) on an interval [a,b].

Wer choose n+1 points xi, i=0,…,n, in the interval [a,b] such that xi≠xj and construct interpolation polynomial through the points Ti=(xi,f(xi)).

Theorem. For every x∈[a,b] the error bound holds (we assume that f is n+1 times differentiable:)

f(x)−pn(x)=ω(x)(n+1)!f(n+1)(ξ),ω(x)=∏k=0n(x−xk)=(x−x0)(x−x1)⋯(x−xn),ξ∈(a,b).

Proof. (See Numerička matematika, p. 23.)

For x=xi, the statement is obvious. Let x≠xi. We define auxilliary function

g(y)=f(y)−pn(y)−kω(y),

where the constant k is chosen such that g(x)=0. In this manner g has at least n+2 zeros, g(x)=g(x0)=g(x1)=⋯=g(xn)=0. By Rolle theorem, the derivative g′ nas at least n+1 zeros, g″ has at least n zeros, etc. The function g(n+1) has at least one zero ξ∈(a,b).

It holds pn(n+1)(y)=0 and ω(n+1)(y)=(n+1)! (the leading coefficient of ω(x) is 1). Inserting gives

0=g(n+1)(ξ)=f(n+1)(ξ)−k(n+1)!

so k=f(n+1)(ξ)(n+1)!, which proves the theorem.

Chebyshev points

Chebyshev polynomial Tn(x) is the polynomial of order n given by the formula

Tn(x)=cos⁡(narccos⁡x),n=0,1,2,…

Trigonometric addition formula

cos⁡(n+1)φ+cos⁡(n−1)φ=2cos⁡φcos⁡nφ

with φ=arccos⁡x implies recursive formulas:

T0(x)=1,T1(x)=x,Tn+1(x)=2xTn(x)−Tn−1(x),n=1,2,…

For example,

T2(x)=2x2−1,T3(x)=4x3−3x,…

The zeros of Tn(x) are

xk=cos⁡(2k−12nπ),k=1,2,…,n,

and they all lie interval [−1,1]. In the points

ξk=cos⁡(kπn),k=0,1,2,…,n,

Tn(x)

attains alternately local maxima and minima 1 and −1, respectively, on the interval [−1,1].

On the interval [−1,1], the polynomial Tn(x) attains values in the interval [−1,1].

Norms of functions

For functions

f,g:[a,b]→R

define scalar product (or dot product) as

(f,g)=∫abf(x)g(x)dx

and the weighted scalar product with weight ω(x)>0 as

(f,g)ω=∫abf(x)g(x)ω(x)dx.

Functions f and g are orthogonal if (f,g)=0 or if (f,g)ω=0.

The following three norms are natural generalizations of the corresponding vector norms:

∥f∥2=(f,f)=∫abf2(x)dx∥f∥1=∫ab|f(x)|dx∥f∥∞=maxx∈[a,b]|f(x)|

12.2 μs

We have the following important theorem:

Theorem. Of all polynomials of degree at most n with leading coefficient equal to 1, the polynomial 12n−1Tn(x) has the smallest norm ∥⋅∥∞ on the interval [−1,1] and this norm is exactly 12n−1.

Proof : (See Numerička matematika, p. 95.)

The coefficient of the power xn of the Chebyshev polynomial Tn(x) equals 2n−1. Therefore, coefficient of the power xn of the polynomial 12n−1Tn(x) is 1. This and the properties of Chebishev polanomials imply

|12n−1Tn(x)|≤12n−1

so

∥12n−1Tn(x)∥∞=12n−1.

Assume that the polynomial

pn(x)=xn+αn−1xn−1+αn−2xn−2+⋯α1x+α0

satisfies |pn(x)|<12n−1 for each x∈[−1,1]. Let ξ0,ξ1,…,ξn be the points where Tn(x) attains extremal values −1 or 1. Then, we must have

pn(ξ0)<12n−1Tn(ξ0)=12n−1pn(ξ1)>12n−1Tn(ξ1)=−12n−1pn(ξ2)<12n−1Tn(ξ2)=12n−1⋮

This imples that the polynomial pn(x)−12n−1Tn(x) of degree at most n−1 changes its sign n times, which is impossibe. This completes the proof.

We conclude that the polynomial approximation (1) on the interval [−1,1] will be the best in ∞-norm if we choose

ω(x)=12nTn+1(x).

Thus, on the interval [a,b] for interpolation points x0,x1,…,xn we shall choose zeros of Tn+1(x) mapped to the interval [a,b].
